Create two-dimensional and three-dimensional sketches, including rough and refined sketches, demonstrating shape, volume, depth, and dimension. Distinguish among common illustration techniques used in design composition such as one-point, two-point, and multi-point perspective drawings. Develop conceptual design ideas using freehand sketching. For a given design problem, generate, analyze, and refine sketches to develop design solutions. Use the sketches to create refined drawings utilizing design software. For example, create thumbnail sketches to generate ideas for a logo or advertisement. (TN Reading 2, 3, 4, 7; TN Writing 4, 6, 7, 8, 9)

Sketchbook Prompts

This section is currently being populated. Some of the prompts are there and ready for your viewing. However, about half the list's descriptions are not yet in place. If you're interested in any of the work that is not yet detailed on the page, please come see me. Also, if at any point, you're interested in something specific being listed as one of your sketchbook assignments, and it's not in this list, pitch your idea to me, and it may end up becoming an option for the weekly topics.

Pick any 2 topics from the list below and complete them within your sketchbook. Make sure that for each one you do, you dedicate and fill a whole page with your practice. A single miniscule drawing on a page will not count towards the credit for your sketchbook!

This assignment is about taking something recognizeable and making it less so in a way that makes sense to you. An easy way to do so is listed as your recommended prompt below, but it is not the only way. You are encouraged to experiment, just like with any other prompt.

  • Take a certain portion of a subject of your choosing, and draw a magnified (zoomed in) version of that particular thing.

Choose a book, music album, or video game cover and do a detailed analysis of it's composition. Make note of how different subjects of the cover are illustrated, rendered, or photographed and their placement in relation to one another. If it's an illustrated cover, break down the art style, indicating what things make it look unique compared to other works of art of a similar nature.

Choose either or both of the prompts that follow this paragraph. The purpose of these exercises is to get you practicing your reading comprehension, writing skills, and spelling, while exposing you to common or uncommon font styles used in media. While doing either of the options, make note of any new words that you come across, as well as their definitions. Try to incoroporate any new words you learn into your future sketchbook writings.

  1. Font Analysis: Look up the font that is utilized within the article. Break each letter of that font down into its parts, making note of what particular things are unique to making up that font's appearance.
  2. Handwritten Quote: Choose a quote from within an article that interests you. Rewrite the quote in a style of handwriting that interests you, paying attention to the different nuances of that style.

Draw one or more things on the page, paying attention to their placement. The goal is to create a good balance of positive and negative space in your sketch. Research compositional rules to try out, if you're struggling with the concept.

This is similar to journaling about your dreams. However, instead of just writing, you're putting an emphasis on illustrating what occurred in your dream. You can still add writing if that's what you wish to do. It isn't required in particular prompt, though. This one is more visually focused.

These practices are centered around improving your basic illustration techniques. If, at any point, you don't know what to do or draw for a specific prompts, these are always a good fallback.

  • Stability Practice: See resources section.
  • Pressure Control Practice: See resources section.
  • Line Commitment/Confidence Exercise: Practice drawing in pen, marker, or some other medium that you can't erase, while making smooth, consistent lines to accomplish a drawing. The point of this is that you have to commit to what's on the paper, so it helps you to both accept and learn from your mistakes. Many people have issues with this, so this is a common practice for anyone trying to really improve their artistic skills.

Choose a handwriting style that you find interesting. If you're looking at a font, check to see if it's based on a particular writing style that is already established.

  1. Do a set of each of the practice strokes for that style going from one side of the page to at least the midway point of the page, from left to right.
  2. Practice writing both the capital and lowercase letter forms while paying attention to the stroke order necessary for that particular writing style.
  3. Optionally, practice writing that style in another language, so you're less focused on the words and more on the letters that comprise each word.

This one tends to make people uncomfortable, but bear with me here. It's your notebook. Just start writing whatever comes to mind.

It should go without saying that a journal is where you can record daily events in your life or just write down things that are on your mind. This is a good exercise for not only maintaining your mental health, but logical thought sequencing as well. The more you write and spend time formulating your thoughts in a more deliberate manner, the better you will be at forming the thoughts needed to communicate your ideas or your perspective on different things. Overall, it just works, so I'm going to provide you with a list of things to use to start with. You can write about any or all of them, regularly, but ultimately, you decide what you write.

  • Write about three things you're grateful for.
  • Write about three things you would like to improve in your life, whether it be a personal trait or some sort of controllable circumstance.
    • I specify "controllable" because a lot of what people tend to have issues with are the things in their life that are outside of their control, but by focusing on the things that are within your control, you can reduce the amount of stress and anxiety in your everyday life.
  • Write about what happened throughout the day.
  • Make observations about what's around you.
  • Write about a problem, internal or external, that you're having and need to work out a solution to.
  • For example: You have an argument with someone, and instead of involving a third party in your business, you write out your thoughts on the issue in an effort to come up with a solution, instead of approaching the individual while you're still emotionally charged.
  • Alternatively: You have a problem with overspending and you want to work out a budget to save money. Write down your average income, drawing on your last 3 to 5 paychecks, and start subtracting fixed expenses, the things that stay the same price each month. Afterward, get the average of your variable expenses, things that change on a month-to-month basis (like groceries, gas, etc.), from the last 3 to 5 months, and subtract that from your total monthly income to get your net income.

Make a copy of or cut out a magazine spread, single page, or article, and perform an analysis of its overall composition. Pay attention to the way images are placed alongside text, the types and sizes of fonts that exist within the piece, and the color choices utilized on the page(s). Notate your findings and write your thoughts an opinions on it.

Pick a masterwork at your own discretion, whether it be an old one or someone you consider a master of a contemporary style of art. Recreate their work to the best of your ability, making note of anything you learn about how their work was composed as you go along. Additionally, make note of anything you couldn't figure out how to do while you were working on your recreation of the piece.
